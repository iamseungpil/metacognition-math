\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

Our experiments answer four questions, each with a dedicated table:
does \pmishift{} improve accuracy over a confound-free baseline (RQ1,
Table~\ref{tab:t1}); where does any effect come from (RQ2,
Table~\ref{tab:t2}); how does it vary with difficulty, domain, and
distribution shift (RQ3, Table~\ref{tab:t3}); and what happens to
calibration (RQ4, Table~\ref{tab:t4}). This draft precedes the final
gs300 evaluation: every result cell is a placeholder, and the few
numbers we do quote are training-time or small-$n$ signals explicitly
marked \emph{preliminary}.

\subsection{Setup and evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

\paragraph{Models and training.} All arms start from Qwen3-8B. The
treatment arm follows the pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:method}: SFT-1
primes \metaopen{}\dots\metaclose{} blocks inside the reasoning trace
(\texttt{v8\_meta\_inside\_strict} data), SFT-2 adds functional
redirect/verify behaviors (\texttt{rv\_functional}), and RL is a
GRPO-family objective (\texttt{TRIOBJ\_DCPO\_V4})~\citep{shao2024deepseekmath}
with \pmishift{} as the auxiliary meta-region reward
(Section~\ref{sec:method:pmishift}): the correctness reward
$R_{\mathrm{corr}} \in \{-1,+1\}$ (\texttt{math\_verify}) is the primary
objective with weight $w_{\mathrm{corr}} = 1.0$, and the meta-region reward
$R_{\mathrm{shift}}$ has weight $w_{\mathrm{meta}} = 0.8$, linearly warmed up
over the first 80 steps, alongside the protocol-maintenance heads and costs
of Section~\ref{sec:method:pmishift} ($w_{\mathrm{cal}} = 0.3$,
$w_{\mathrm{format}} = 0.35$, $w_{\mathrm{emit}} = 0.1$, length cost $0.08$,
truncation penalty $0.3$). The control arm is the matched-base twin
of Section~\ref{sec:method:twin}: identical data with meta blocks
stripped, identical non-meta hyperparameters (byte-audited config diff),
and vanilla GRPO with correctness-only reward. Both arms are trained with a
4{,}096-token response cap (identical across arms) and evaluated at 16k
(below); the token-budget control column and tokens-per-response reporting
in Table~\ref{tab:t1} make any train/eval length-extrapolation asymmetry
visible rather than hidden.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.} We evaluate on a held-out suite of
1{,}030 problems: 500 GSM8K, 500 MATH500, and 30 AIME. Answers are
graded with \texttt{math\_verify}; generation uses a 16k max-token
budget at temperature $0.7$. We report avg@8 accuracy (avg@16 on AIME
to reduce variance on the small set). Both arms are always evaluated
\emph{in the same job with identical seeds}, so any run-to-run
infrastructure variation is shared across arms. Main results use 3
training seeds (mean$\pm$std), and Table~\ref{tab:t1} includes a
token-budget control column (tokens per response) so that accuracy
differences cannot be silently explained by one arm simply generating
longer.

\paragraph{Stratified reporting.} All meta-vs-no-meta comparisons are
reported \emph{difficulty-stratified}, never only in aggregate. The
reason is a Simpson-style artifact we document in
Section~\ref{sec:exp:rq3}: meta-block emission is strongly
difficulty-dependent (preliminary: $\approx$10\% on the easiest quartile
vs.\ $\approx$98\% on the hardest), so conditioning on ``response
contains a meta block'' selects hard problems, and aggregate
per-response comparisons mechanically make meta responses look worse.

\subsection{RQ1: Does \pmishift{} improve held-out accuracy?}
\label{sec:exp:rq1}

\paragraph{Question and design.} RQ1 asks whether adding the
\pmishift{} meta-shaping reward to an otherwise identical GRPO run
improves end-task accuracy. Table~\ref{tab:t1} compares the matched
base twin against \pmishift{} at the final training checkpoint (gs300),
with raw Qwen3-8B and SFT-only rows as reference points that locate the
SFT capability cost (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}) and the RL gain
separately, plus per-domain columns and the token-budget control.

\paragraph{Decision rule.} \pmishift{} is supported if its overall
held-out accuracy exceeds the matched base by more than one pooled
standard deviation across the 3 seeds \emph{without} a commensurate
increase in tokens per response; it is refuted if the gap is within
noise or is fully absorbed by the token-budget control. Because the
twin differs from the treatment only in the meta package of
Section~\ref{sec:method:twin}, any significant gap is attributable to
that package as a whole; apportioning it to the \pmishift{} reward
specifically is the job of RQ2. As a preliminary,
non-final signal: \pmishift{} led the matched base at all 8 same-step
validation checkpoints during training (gap $+0.02$ to $+0.12$,
narrowing late), measured on val-aux \texttt{math\_verify} correctness,
the only cross-arm-comparable validation metric (the composite
validation reward is meta-shaped and excluded from arm comparisons);
the gs300 held-out judgment is pending and this
training-time signal must not be read as a result.

\subsection{RQ2: Where does the effect come from?}
\label{sec:exp:rq2}

\paragraph{Question and design.} If \pmishift{} helps, the gain could
come from three places: the SFT priming of meta behaviors, the RL
reward that selectively reinforces them, or a non-semantic artifact of
emitting extra tokens (a ``pause'' effect). Table~\ref{tab:t2}
decomposes these with three probes. \emph{(i) Gandhi arm:} meta-SFT
priming followed by \emph{vanilla} GRPO (correctness-only), directly
instantiating the hypothesis of \citet{gandhi2025cognitive} that RL
amplifies whatever behaviors priming installs --- it isolates the value
of priming without the \pmishift{} reward. \emph{(ii) Flip analysis:}
using the 4-arm teacher-forced PMI probe of
Section~\ref{sec:method:pmishift}, we measure how often the model's
evidence flips around a meta block: decoy$\to$gold (SAVE) vs.\
gold$\to$decoy (DERAIL), per arm. \emph{(iii) Placebo:} meta-block
\emph{content} is shuffled across problems at evaluation time while
positions and lengths are preserved, testing whether the content of the
block, rather than its mere presence, carries the effect.

\paragraph{Decision rule.} The \pmishift{} \emph{reward} (beyond
priming) is supported if \pmishift{} $>$ Gandhi arm on accuracy and on
net flips (SAVE $-$ DERAIL); if Gandhi $\approx$ \pmishift{}, the
mechanism reduces to SFT priming amplified by generic RL. The
\emph{semantic} interpretation is supported only if the placebo arm
loses most of the SAVE-rate and accuracy advantage; if shuffled content
performs comparably, the meta block is a computational pause, not
functional metacognition.

\subsection{RQ3: Difficulty, domain, and OOD stratification}
\label{sec:exp:rq3}

\paragraph{Question and design.} Metacognition should pay where the
model is actually uncertain; on easy problems a verification detour can
only cost tokens. Table~\ref{tab:t3} therefore stratifies accuracy and
meta-emission rate by difficulty quartile (Q1 easy to Q4 hard, quartiles
defined by the matched base's per-problem pass rate), reports per-domain
breakdowns, and treats AIME and the hardest strata as an
out-of-distribution probe: since \pmishift{} distills the model's
\emph{own} evidence contrast rather than imitating teacher traces, we
frame the OOD rows as a test of whether self-distilled metacognition
transfers where imitation typically degrades~\citep{kim2026selfdistill}.
This table is also where we document the Simpson artifact
quantitatively: because emission tracks difficulty (preliminary:
Q1 $\approx$10\% $\to$ Q4 $\approx$98\%), the aggregate
``responses-with-meta vs.\ without'' comparison is confounded, and only
within-stratum arm-vs-arm comparisons are meaningful.

\paragraph{Decision rule.} The difficulty-dependence hypothesis is
supported if the \pmishift{}-vs-base gap grows monotonically from Q1 to
Q3/Q4 (with Q1 $\approx$ 0, ruling out a uniform token-budget effect)
and if the AIME/hard-domain rows show a non-negative gap; it is refuted
if the gap is flat across quartiles or concentrated on easy strata. As
a preliminary small-$n$ observation only: on mid-hard Q3, responses
with meta blocks scored 0.83 vs.\ 0.67 without --- suggestive of the
predicted pattern, but subject to the very selection confound this
table is designed to remove, and not a result.

\subsection{RQ4: Calibration as a secondary outcome}
\label{sec:exp:rq4}

\paragraph{Question and design.} Our position
(Section~\ref{sec:related:calibration}) is that calibration should be
subordinate to functional metacognition, not its target. We must be
precise about what the launched run optimizes: the treatment objective
\emph{does} include an explicit Brier-style calibration head on the
confidence token ($w_{\mathrm{cal}} = 0.3$;
Section~\ref{sec:method:pmishift}), so calibration is not a pure
byproduct here. RQ4 therefore asks two things: (i) whether the
treatment ends up better calibrated than the matched base at equal or
better accuracy, and (ii) whether the \emph{functional} meta reward
contributes calibration benefit beyond the explicit calibration term
--- to be answered by a planned $w_{\mathrm{cal}} = 0$ ablation arm
added to Table~\ref{tab:t4}. Table~\ref{tab:t4} reports, per
arm, expected calibration error (15-bin ECE~\citep{guo2017calibration}),
Brier score, and overconfidence rate on the held-out suite. Accuracy
(RQ1) remains the primary outcome; these metrics are strictly
secondary.

\paragraph{Decision rule.} The functional-metacognition framing is
supported if \pmishift{} improves ECE/overconfidence relative to the
matched base \emph{at equal or better accuracy}, and if the
$w_{\mathrm{cal}} = 0$ ablation retains most of that calibration gain
(showing the functional reward, not the explicit Brier term, carries
it); calibration gains
purchased with accuracy losses would instead replicate the known
calibration-accuracy trade-off and refute our claim that
problem-solving-directed metacognition subsumes it. No change in
calibration with an accuracy gain would be a neutral outcome: it would
weaken the secondary-outcome story without touching the primary claim.
