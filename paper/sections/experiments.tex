\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We ask four questions. RQ1: does the meta package raise held-out accuracy
over an otherwise identical run? RQ2: which part of the package carries the
effect? RQ3: does the gain live where the model is actually uncertain, and
does meta emission track it? RQ4: what happens to calibration? Each question
follows the same order: the plain-language intent, the experiment that
answers it, the result, and the interpretation.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:exp:setup}

Every result below shares one evaluation protocol, and we state its caveats
here so that later paragraphs do not repeat them. The numbers come from a
single training seed at one checkpoint (gs300) and are therefore
preliminary. Answers are scored by a robust \texttt{math\_verify} re-grade,
which replaces an earlier brittle grader that had marked some correct
answers wrong. The held-out suite is 1{,}030 problems: 500 from GSM8K, 500
from MATH500, and 30 from AIME. For each problem we sample several answers
and average their correctness; we report this average over 8 samples (over
16 samples on the small AIME set, to reduce noise). We evaluate at two
generation budgets, a 4k-token cap and a 16k-token cap, so that any length
effect is visible. The treatment arm is a seven-head reward package, not the
meta-shift reward alone. Its per-head decomposition is the subject of RQ2;
until then, every treatment-minus-base gap is the effect of the whole
package. We tag each result and caption ``(prelim., package)'' as a reminder
of these two facts.

The remaining setup is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:setup}. The two arms are
byte-identical twins: same base model, same data, and same non-meta
hyperparameters, differing only in whether meta blocks are present and
rewarded. This is what lets us read any accuracy gap as the package effect.
The base arm emits no meta block and no confidence token at all, an
asymmetry that matters in RQ4.

\subsection{RQ1: Does the meta package improve held-out accuracy?}
\label{sec:exp:rq1}

\paragraph{Intent.} The core question is whether rewarding metacognitive acts
actually raises end-task accuracy. Rewarding them is only worthwhile if a run
that does so beats an otherwise identical run that does not.

\paragraph{Method.} We compare the byte-identical twins on the same held-out
problems at the same checkpoint. One arm carries the meta package; the other
is vanilla GRPO with a correctness-only reward. The two arms are graded in the
same job with the same seeds, so any gap between them is the package. We test
each gap two ways. A problem-paired bootstrap resamples the held-out problems
with replacement and checks whether the paired accuracy gap stays above zero;
this yields a bootstrap $p$ and a 95\% interval. McNemar's test asks whether
the two arms disagree asymmetrically on the same problems, which is
informative on the tiny 30-problem AIME set.

\paragraph{Result.} The package wins on all three domains, and the margin grows
with difficulty (Table~\ref{tab:t1}). On GSM8K the gap is small but reliable,
$+4.0$ points. On MATH500 it is large, $+18.8$ points. On AIME it is
$+14.2$ points, from a base accuracy of only $5.0$. The bootstrap places every
gap above zero with $p<.001$; McNemar agrees except on the smallest AIME cell,
where it is borderline. A second result concerns the token budget
(Table~\ref{tab:t1b}). Both arms are flat from 4k to 16k: extra tokens buy no
accuracy on either side. What changes with budget is truncation, meaning how
often a response runs into the length cap without finishing. The base arm
truncates far more, about 19\% of MATH500 responses and 73\% of AIME responses,
against about 6\% and 50\% for the meta arm. The base arm emits no meta blocks
at any budget.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The gain is not paid for with tokens, since neither
arm improves when given four times the budget. That rules out the simplest
alternative explanation, that meta blocks merely license longer generations.
The truncation numbers point the other way. Long-budget degeneration, where a
response fills the budget without terminating, is a property of the base policy
that the meta mechanism mitigates rather than causes. The meta arm finishes
more often on exactly the hard problems where the base arm stalls.

\subsection{RQ2: Where does the effect come from? (pending)}
\label{sec:exp:rq2}

\paragraph{Intent.} The RQ1 gap is a package effect, and a package can win for
uninteresting reasons. Four candidates compete. The meta-SFT priming alone
might carry it. The meta-shift reward, which credits meta blocks that move the
model's answer toward the correct one, might add value on top of priming. The
blocks might work only as a computational pause, with their content irrelevant.
Or the reward might farm boilerplate that echoes the surrounding reasoning, the
shortcut failure that self-distillation is known to invite~\citep{shen2026antisd}.

\paragraph{Method.} We pre-register four arms, each named by its function, and
fix a decision rule for each before the run. A priming-only arm applies meta-SFT
and then correctness-only GRPO, with no shift reward. A shift-only arm adds the
meta-shift reward on top of priming but turns the other maintenance heads off,
isolating the shaping reward. A shuffled-content placebo keeps each meta block's
position and length but scrambles its wording across problems. A duplicate-body
filter arm withholds credit from meta blocks that near-duplicate the preceding
reasoning. Table~\ref{tab:t2} holds the pending cells.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Each rule states in advance what a given outcome
would prove, which is the point of pre-registration. If the shift-only arm beats
the priming-only arm, the shift reward adds value beyond priming; if the two tie,
the reward is inert and the win reduces to priming. If the shift-only arm beats
the shuffled-content placebo, the block's meaning carries the effect; if they
tie, the block is a pause, not functional metacognition. If the duplicate-body
filter barely changes the shift-only accuracy, the reward is not farming
boilerplate; a large drop would concede the shortcut diagnosis. The design thus
converts a single package number into a set of falsifiable claims.

\subsection{RQ3: Where does the gain live, and does emission track it?}
\label{sec:exp:rq3}

\paragraph{Intent.} Metacognition should pay off where the model is genuinely
uncertain, not everywhere. If it does, the gain should concentrate on hard
problems, and the model should ideally spend meta effort there rather than on
easy ones.

\paragraph{Method.} We sort the held-out problems into difficulty quartiles,
from Q1 (easiest) to Q4 (hardest), using the base arm's per-problem pass rate as
the difficulty score. Within each quartile we compare the two arms' accuracy,
and we also record how often the meta arm emits a meta block. Emission rate and
accuracy gain can then be read side by side.

\paragraph{Result.} The gain is concentrated on hard problems (Table~\ref{tab:t3}).
On Q1 and Q2 both arms are near 100\%, so there is no room to improve. On Q3 the
meta arm scores $95.7$ against the base's $84.6$, a gap of $+11.1$ points. On Q4
it scores $46.9$ against $12.1$, a gap of $+34.8$ points, close to a fourfold
relative improvement. Emission tells a different story. The meta arm emits a
block on 86\% to 96\% of responses in every quartile, roughly constant across
difficulty.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} Emission and payoff dissociate. The model verbalizes
metacognition at nearly the same rate everywhere, but that metacognition only
converts to accuracy on hard problems. This has two consequences. First,
aggregate comparisons understate the effect, because easy quartiles that are
already saturated dilute the large hard-quartile gain. Second, uniform emission
is an efficiency cost, since the model spends meta tokens on easy problems where
they cannot help. The hardest cells, including AIME, may also hint that the
learned metacognition holds up somewhat away from the training distribution,
though we do not press this point.

\subsection{RQ4: What happens to calibration?}
\label{sec:exp:rq4}

\paragraph{Intent.} We treat calibration as subordinate to functional
metacognition, not as the target. Still, since the meta arm states a confidence
level, it is fair to ask how honest those statements are.

\paragraph{Method.} We report calibration for the meta arm only, because the base
arm emits no confidence token and so cannot be scored on it. We use expected
calibration error, which is the average gap between a stated confidence level and
the actual accuracy of answers made at that confidence, computed over 15 bins. We
also report the overconfidence rate, the share of problems where the model states
high confidence yet answers wrong.

\paragraph{Result.} The meta arm is well-calibrated on the easier domains and
poorly calibrated on the hardest (Table~\ref{tab:t4}). Expected calibration error
is $0.082$ on GSM8K and $0.052$ on MATH500, with a pooled value of $0.053$. On
AIME it rises to $0.402$, and the model is overconfident on $81.8\%$ of problems.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} This is a candid negative for the calibration story.
The confidence statements are trustworthy on GSM8K and MATH500 but collapse on
AIME, where the model asserts high confidence on problems it gets wrong. Accuracy
remains the primary outcome, and the AIME breakdown motivates an ablation that
turns the explicit calibration head off, to test whether any calibration benefit
comes from the functional meta reward rather than from that head.
