\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) has become the dominant
recipe for improving mathematical reasoning in large language models: GRPO and
its descendants optimize a single scalar signal---final-answer
correctness---over sampled solution traces
\citep{shao2024deepseekmath,deepseekr1,zeng2025simplerlzoo,qwen3}. This recipe
is remarkably effective at sharpening the policy toward answers the model can
already reach, but it leaves a conspicuous gap: nothing in the objective
teaches the model to \emph{handle its own uncertainty}. Trained models remain
overconfident, commit to a flawed line of attack early, and rarely notice
mid-solution that their evidence no longer supports the path they are on
\citep{huang2024llmcannot,kadavath2022language}. When a problem exceeds the
model's reliable competence, the trace does not slow down, verify, or
redirect---it simply produces a fluent wrong answer.

The field's default remedy for this failure mode is scale: larger models, more
RL compute, longer traces. Scaling raises the competence frontier, but it does
not change the character of behavior \emph{at} the frontier---wherever a
model's reliable reach ends, the same silent overconfidence reappears. What is
missing is not more capability but a mechanism for the model to detect and act
on the boundary of its own capability during a solution.

Two recent lines of evidence diagnose why RLVR alone does not supply this
mechanism. \citet{gandhi2025cognitive} show that RL amplifies cognitive
behaviors---verification, backtracking, subgoal setting, backward
chaining---only when those behaviors are already present in the base model or
its priming data; RL selects among existing behaviors rather than installing
new ones. \citet{kim2026selfdistill} show the complementary failure from the
self-distillation side: distilling a model's own outputs back into itself can
suppress epistemic content and \emph{degrade} reasoning, and their follow-up
remedy \citep{kim2026rebellious}, which drives exploration by contrasting
against a frozen teacher's signals, suggests that a frozen model's
likelihoods carry a usable contrast signal that the plain objective
lacks. Read together, these results say that
current pipelines reward the \emph{form} of self-reflective reasoning wherever
it happens to appear, but never its \emph{function}. A model can learn to emit
verification-shaped text---``let me double-check this step''---while the text
changes nothing about the answer, because no term in the objective asks
whether the reflection moved the model's evidence.

Functional metacognition, by contrast, would require three things: an explicit,
inspectable locus for metacognitive acts (so they can be rewarded separately
from object-level reasoning); a reward that measures whether each act
\emph{causally improves the model's evidence about the answer}, not merely
whether it is emitted; and an evaluation design that can attribute any
downstream accuracy gain to the metacognitive mechanism rather than to
confounded data or hyperparameters. Calibration research supplies part of the
picture---models do carry usable uncertainty signals
\citep{kadavath2022language,lin2022teaching,tian2023just}---but knowing
uncertainty is not the same as using it: what is missing is training pressure
that converts uncertainty into problem-solving actions such as verifying and
redirecting \citep{zelikman2022star,kumar2024score}.

We build exactly this. First, we define in-reasoning metacognition
operationally: the model emits \metaopen{}\dots\metaclose{} blocks inside its
chain of thought, primed by two SFT stages that teach where such blocks belong
and what functional content (verify, redirect) they should carry. Second, we
reinforce each block only by what it accomplishes. Our \pmishift{} reward
freezes a reference model and teacher-forces the gold answer and a rule-based
decoy at two points---just before \metaopen{} and just after
\metaclose{}---yielding a pointwise-mutual-information contrast at each point;
the reward is the \emph{shift} in this contrast across the block, plus a
reversal bonus when the block flips the preferred answer from decoy to gold
(and a penalty for the reverse). The shift is routed onto the meta-block
tokens alone, so the meta region is rewarded exactly insofar as it moves the
model's own gold-vs-decoy evidence: the model distills its latent evidence
contrast into explicit metacognitive text---metacognitive self-distillation.
Correctness remains the primary objective; \pmishift{} is an auxiliary shaping
term. Third, we train a matched-base twin---identical data with meta content
stripped, byte-audited identical non-meta hyperparameters, correctness-only
GRPO---so that any difference between arms isolates the meta mechanism itself.
All experimental results in this draft are pending (\todo{T1--T4}); as a
preliminary signal, the \pmishift{} arm leads its matched twin at every
same-step validation checkpoint observed so far, and meta-block emission
tracks problem difficulty (preliminary; final held-out judgments pending).

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item An \textbf{operational, executable definition of in-reasoning
  metacognition}: explicit \metaopen{}\dots\metaclose{} blocks governing when to
  verify and redirect, paired with a matched-twin causal design that isolates
  the contribution of the meta mechanism from data and hyperparameter
  confounds.
\item \textbf{\pmishift{}}, a metacognitive self-distillation reward that
  scores each meta block by the gold-vs-decoy evidence shift it produces in
  the model's own likelihoods, rewarding metacognitive function rather than
  form.
\item An \textbf{analysis suite}---priming-vs-reward decomposition, flip
  (save/derail) analysis, placebo meta content, difficulty stratification, and
  calibration---designed to locate \emph{where} metacognition pays: testing
  whether it acts as a difficulty-dependent and out-of-distribution mechanism
  rather than a uniform accuracy additive.
\item An \textbf{empirical characterization on math reasoning}
  (GSM8K/MATH500/AIME, Qwen3-8B), including a Simpson-artifact analysis:
  preliminary evidence indicates meta emission concentrates on hard problems,
  implying that aggregate meta-vs-base comparisons can understate the effect
  and must be difficulty-stratified (final stratified results pending).
\end{enumerate}
