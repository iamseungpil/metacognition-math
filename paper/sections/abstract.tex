\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards has become the standard recipe
for training reasoning models, yet the resulting models handle their own
uncertainty poorly: they are overconfident, rarely notice mid-solution errors,
and the field's default remedy is to scale the model. Recent evidence suggests
RL amplifies surface behaviors already present in the base model without
installing \emph{functional} metacognition---self-assessment that actually
solves more problems. We define in-reasoning metacognition operationally as
explicit \metaopen{}\dots\metaclose{} blocks (deciding when to verify or change
approach) and reinforce each block only by the evidence shift it causes:
\pmishift{} scores a meta block by how much it moves the model's own
log-likelihood contrast between the gold answer and a decoy, a form of
metacognitive self-distillation. A matched-base twin---identical data with
meta content stripped, byte-audited hyperparameters, correctness-only
GRPO---isolates the causal contribution. On GSM8K, MATH500, and AIME with
Qwen3-8B we report main accuracy (\todo{T1}), a mechanism decomposition
separating SFT priming from RL reward (\todo{T2}), difficulty- and
OOD-stratified effects (\todo{T3}), and calibration outcomes (\todo{T4}),
together with a Simpson-artifact warning arguing, with preliminary evidence,
that aggregate meta-vs-base comparisons can systematically mislead.
\end{abstract}
