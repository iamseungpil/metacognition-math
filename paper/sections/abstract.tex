\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards now trains language models into
strong mathematical reasoners, yet these models still handle their own
uncertainty badly. They stay overconfident, and they rarely catch their own
errors mid-solution. Making the models larger does not change this. The two
obvious repairs both fail. Training a model on its own correct solutions, a
procedure called self-distillation, can instead degrade its reasoning. Teaching
the model to report a calibrated confidence lowered its overconfidence but did
not restore its accuracy. We argue for a different target: a metacognitive act
should be rewarded for the causal effect it has on the model's evidence about
the answer, not for the confidence the model reports. We turn this into a
concrete method. The model writes explicit metacognitive blocks inside its
reasoning, and we score each block by how far it shifts the model's own evidence
away from a plausible wrong answer and toward the correct one. A twin model,
trained on byte-identical settings with the metacognitive content removed,
isolates this mechanism. On held-out mathematics with Qwen3-8B, the
metacognitive model beats its matched twin on all six benchmark comparisons,
improving GSM8K by $4.0$, MATH500 by $18.8$, and AIME by $14.2$ percentage
points (preliminary, single seed, package-level), with the gain concentrated on
the hardest problems.
\end{abstract}
