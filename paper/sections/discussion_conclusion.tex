\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Absolute accuracy and the SFT capability cost.}
Our pipeline requires two SFT stages to install the \metaopen{}\dots\metaclose{}
protocol before RL, and SFT priming is known to carry a capability cost
relative to RL applied directly to the base model. Consequently, our absolute
numbers should not be read against no-SFT RLVR baselines: on Qwen3-8B-Base
without SFT, \citet{kim2026rebellious} report GRPO at 83.6 on MATH500 and 19.8
on AIME24, and their RLRT (``Rebellious Student'') variant at 84.4 and 27.9
respectively (20{,}480-token budget,
avg@16).\footnote{These four numbers are quoted from the cited preprint and
have not been independently reproduced by us.}
The claim this paper is designed to support is not state-of-the-art accuracy
but the \emph{causal} contribution of the meta mechanism, read off the
matched-base twin that shares data, hyperparameters, and the same SFT cost
(Section~\ref{sec:method}); the raw-Qwen3-8B and SFT-only reference rows in
Table~\ref{tab:t1} make the priming cost explicit rather than hiding it.

\paragraph{Verify-boilerplate in the SFT data.}
The current functional-verify SFT corpus contains boilerplate verification
phrasing: many verify blocks re-assert the preceding step rather than perform
an independent check, rewarding the \emph{form} of verification over its
\emph{function}. RL on top of such a prime can amplify the template
\citep{gandhi2025cognitive} even when \pmishift{} scores evidence movement. A
v2 corpus with R1-style annotation --- meta blocks distilled from traces where
verification demonstrably changed the trajectory --- is planned; the placebo
arm in Table~\ref{tab:t2} bounds how much of any observed gain the boilerplate
alone can explain.

\paragraph{Token budget.}
Meta blocks consume tokens, so any meta-vs-base comparison is confounded with
inference-time compute. Some of our legacy evaluations used a 4k-token budget
under which meta traces were disproportionately truncated; those numbers are
superseded by the 16k protocol of Section~\ref{sec:exp:setup}, and
Table~\ref{tab:t1} carries a token-budget control column so the reader can
separate ``thinks better'' from ``thinks longer.''

\paragraph{Scope and measurement.}
All experiments use a single model family and scale (Qwen3-8B) and a single
domain (competition-style math); whether \pmishift{} transfers across scales,
families, or to non-verifiable domains is untested. Grading depends on
\texttt{math\_verify}: an earlier grader incorrectly rejected a substantial
fraction of correct base-model answers, and although the pipeline was audited
after that failure, residual grader asymmetries between meta-bearing and plain
traces cannot be fully excluded. Finally, the \pmishift{} signal itself
depends on a frozen reference model and rule-based decoys; a decoy family that
is too easy, or systematically correlated with the model's own answer, would
let the reward be satisfied without genuine self-verification --- which is why
the SAVE/DERAIL flip rates are reported alongside accuracy.

\paragraph{Threats to validity.}
Internally, the twin design controls what we can byte-audit, but it cannot
control emergent differences that the reward itself induces in exploration
(e.g., entropy trajectories); we therefore compare same-step, same-seed
checkpoints rather than best-checkpoint selections. Statistically, meta
emission and payoff dissociate (preliminary): emission is
\emph{non-selective}---86--94\% of responses carry a meta block across all
difficulty quartiles---while the accuracy benefit concentrates on the
hardest problems. Any within-model comparison of meta-bearing vs.\ meta-free
responses therefore conditions on the endogenous emit decision and is
confounded; the reason all headline comparisons in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments} are between-arm and difficulty-stratified. The
uniform emission on easy problems, where a verification detour cannot help,
is itself an efficiency limitation we flag for future selective-emission work.

\paragraph{What a negative result would still establish.}
If Table~\ref{tab:t1} shows no accuracy gain over the matched base, the
analysis suite still answers mechanistic questions the field currently lacks
instruments for: whether explicit meta blocks move the model's own
gold-vs-decoy evidence at all (flip analysis), whether any effect is content-
or form-driven (placebo), whether SFT priming or the RL reward is the inert
component (Gandhi-arm decomposition), and whether apparent meta effects in
aggregate comparisons are stratification artifacts. A clean null with this
apparatus would be a mechanism paper: evidence that RLVR-style rewards, even
when routed precisely onto metacognitive content, do not install functional
self-verification --- sharpening, rather than merely echoing, the skepticism
of \citet{huang2024llmcannot}.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
We proposed an operational definition of in-reasoning metacognition ---
explicit \metaopen{}\dots\metaclose{} blocks that decide when to verify or
redirect --- and \pmishift{}, a metacognitive self-distillation reward that
scores each block by how much it shifts the model's own evidence from a decoy
toward the gold answer, routed onto meta content only and evaluated against a
byte-audited matched-base twin that isolates the causal contribution of the
mechanism. The four-table experimental program (main effect, mechanism
decomposition, difficulty/OOD stratification, calibration) is designed so that
either outcome is informative: a positive result localizes \emph{where}
metacognition pays, while a negative one cleanly attributes the failure to
priming, reward, or content. Results are pending (\todo{finalize once T1--T4
land}); next steps are the R1-style functional verify corpus (SFT~v2) and
extension beyond math to domains where a learned verifier, not an exact
grader, must supply the notion of gold.
